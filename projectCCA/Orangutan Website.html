What are Orangutans?

Orangutans are the largest arboreal mammals that spend the majority of their time in trees. They are distinguished by their distinctive red fur. They can move through the branches thanks to their long, powerful arms and grasping hands and feet.

Orangutans are omnivores, meaning that they eat both vegetables and meat. An Orangutan's diet consist of fruits insects, termites, lizards, honey, nuts, and only rarely eat meat.

Orangutans are highly intelligent mammals that make tools to do various tasks. but orangutan intelligence goes far beyond making simple tools, research in controlled environments has shown that orangutans are self-aware, being one of the few species to recognize their own reflections.

They also display remarkable foresight, planning, and cognition. In one experiment, researchers taught an orangutan to use a straw to extract his favourite fruit soup from a box. That orangutan was later given the choice between a straw or grape that could be eaten right away, and he chose a straw just in case he was given another box of soup.

In another experiment, orangutans figured out how to reach peanuts at the bottom of long tubes by spitting water into them.

Orangutan population is decreasing

Indonesia has the world's highest rate of deforestation, and millions of acres of rain forest are burned annually to support the logging and palm oil industries. Deforestation exposes the 30,000 orangutans remaining in the wild to poachers. They kill the mothers so that the baby orangutans can be sold as exotic pets. But fortunately, the story often doesnt end here. Orphans can be confiscated and given a second chance. At special forest schools, they recover from emotional trauma and continue to develop essential life skills.

Against all odds, these orphans demonstrate incredible resilience and readiness to learn.
